George Washington (1732—1799)

George Washington was an American political leader, military general, statesman, and Founding Father who served as the first President of the United States from 1789 to 1797. He is often called the "Father of His Country" for his central role in the founding of the United States.

Early life & education

George Washington was born on February 22, 1732, into a Virginia planter family. His father, Augustine Washington, died when George was young; he and his half-brother Lawrence were important influences in his upbringing. Washington received a basic education focused on mathematics, surveying, and the practical skills expected of a Virginia gentleman of the era. He never attended college.

In his late teens Washington worked as a surveyor, gaining familiarity with the backcountry of Virginia. His early experiences included service in the Virginia militia during the French and Indian War, where he first gained military experience and recognition.

Military career

Washington served as an officer in Virginia's militia during the French and Indian War (1754–1763). His leadership and reputation grew over the following decades. When conflict erupted between British rule and the Thirteen Colonies, Washington was chosen by the Continental Congress to command the Continental Army in 1775.

As Commander-in-Chief (1775–1783), Washington led American forces through difficult campaigns, including the harsh winter at Valley Forge (1777–1778), and ultimately achieved victory with allied French forces against the British at Yorktown in 1781. His decision to resign command after the war demonstrated commitment to civilian control of the military.

Presidency (1789–1797)

Washington was elected the first President of the United States in 1789 and re-elected in 1792. During his presidency he established many protocols for the new government, created the first presidential cabinet (including Alexander Hamilton as Secretary of the Treasury and Thomas Jefferson as Secretary of State), and signed legislation that set up the federal court system.

Major events during his administration included the passage and ratification of the Bill of Rights (1791), Hamilton's financial programs to manage the national debt, and policy challenges such as the Whiskey Rebellion (1794). Washington sought to keep the United States neutral amid European conflicts, issuing the Neutrality Proclamation of 1793.

He declined to seek a third term and in his famous Farewell Address (1796) warned against political parties and foreign entanglements.

Legacy

Washington set many precedents for the presidency, including the peaceful transfer of power. He is commemorated in countless ways across the United States: the capital city (Washington, D.C.), the state of Washington, Mount Vernon (his plantation home), statues, and on U.S. currency.

Historians recognize his leadership, integrity, and willingness to relinquish power — all important to the emerging American republic. At the same time, modern assessments also grapple with Washington's status as a slaveholder and the contradictions between ideals of liberty and the realities of slavery during his lifetime.

Washington's plantation and home in Virginia. Today it is a site dedicated to preservation and public education.
